# Responsible Psychiatric Discharge Plans

**Subject:** NAMI Iowa 2026 Legislative Priority: Requiring responsible discharge plans for patients who have had a psychiatric hospitalization (HF 385)  
**Peer Solution Blueprint:** Dane County Department of Human Services (Madison, Wisconsin)

---

### I. SITUATION
Iowa House File 385 is a key piece of mental health legislation that passed the House chamber with a unanimous 89-0 bipartisan vote. This total consensus proves the bill is pre-vetted legislation that can break through partisan friction. The current break between legislative sessions is ideal timing to resolve the stalemate holding the bill back in the Senate Health and Human Services Committee before the busy January 2027 session begins.

---

### II. BACKGROUND
The wording of HF 385 mandates that before a facility can discharge an involuntarily committed psychiatric patient, it must coordinate a warm handoff with an Administrative Services Organization (ASO) and provide a mandatory 15-day supply of all prescribed psychiatric and non-psychiatric medications. 

The point of friction is financial: if immediate coverage is unavailable through Medicaid or commercial insurance at discharge, the facility must bill the regional ASO. Legacy hospital networks and state budget directors are locked over immediate cash-flow liabilities and the overhead of running these temporary, uncompensated medication pipelines.

---

### III. ASSESSMENT
To unblock this loop, we look to Madison, Wisconsin (Dane County), a mid-sized Midwestern capital in a political environment similar to Iowa's. Rather than waiting for top-down state bailouts, Dane County created a horizontal, localized framework to handle the transition window:

* **The Local Discretionary Transition Fund:** Dane County utilizes a flexible, county-administered human services budget pool acting as an immediate financial shock absorber to fill 14-to-30 day prescription bridges for individuals exiting an inpatient hold without active coverage.
* **Pre-Discharge Pharmacy Integration:** Case managers route transitional medication authorizations directly to localized pharmacy networks prior to physical discharge, treating the transition as an active window of care.
* **The Fiscal Counter-Argument:** Actuarial models prove an immediate 15-day medication buffer costs an average of 550 to 800 dollars per individual. Conversely, an immediate relapse resulting in an emergency department visit or county jail intake costs local municipal budgets between 2,200 and 4,500 dollars per day.
