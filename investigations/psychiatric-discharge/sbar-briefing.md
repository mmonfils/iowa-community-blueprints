# RESPONSIBLE PSYCHIATRIC DISCHARGE PLANS (HF 385)

**Subject:** Collaborative Interim Mapping for Responsible Psychiatric Discharge Coordination  
**Peer Solution Blueprint:** No documented peer program found matching these criteria.  
**Date of Summary:** June 24, 2026

## I. SITUATION
Iowa House File 385 passed the House chamber with a unanimous 89-0 bipartisan vote. This complete consensus underscores the bill's legislative validity and its capacity to address critical transitional care gaps. The current legislative interim presents an ideal strategic window to engage with key stakeholders, understand regional challenges, and address the administrative friction points currently stalling the bill before the upcoming January legislative session.

## II. BACKGROUND
HF 385 mandates that prior to discharging an involuntarily committed psychiatric patient, treating facilities must coordinate care with a regional Administrative Services Organization (ASO) and provide a mandatory 15-day supply of prescribed medications, encompassing both psychiatric and nonpsychiatric prescriptions.

The primary systemic roadblock is operational and financial. When immediate Medicaid activation or commercial insurance coverage is unavailable at the exact moment of discharge, local hospital facilities face immediate cash-flow liabilities. According to the official Legislative Services Agency (LSA) fiscal note, the average monthly cost for psychotropic medications ranges from $1,100 to $1,600 per patient. Consequently, a mandatory 15-day transitional supply creates a significant uncompensated care liability for regional facilities if a direct reimbursement channel is not clearly coordinated with the regional ASO layer.

## III. ASSESSMENT
A clear systemic friction point exists between the front-end requirements of hospital facilities and the back-end funding streams of regional administrative structures. 

From a social work perspective, leaving this funding gap unaddressed results in disjointed care transitions, which can trigger acute clinical relapses. When medication continuity is interrupted, individuals face vastly higher risks of emergency department boarding, acute behavioral health crises, and immediate interactions with the county jail system. These back-end outcomes not only compromise individual human dignity but also place a far greater financial and operational strain on local municipal emergency budgets than the front-end investment required for transitional medication.

## IV. RECOMMENDATION
1. **Engage in Localized Interim Mapping:** Utilize the legislative interim period to conduct collaborative, relationship-focused outreach with local stakeholders, including regional ASO coordinators, frontline care managers, and hospital network discharge staff.
2. **Document Operational Friction Points:** Map the specific communication, timing, and administrative bottlenecks that delay medication handoffs or care coordination at the point of inpatient discharge.
3. **Organize Collaborative Feedback Loops:** Synthesize qualitative insights from the field into an approachable, data-grounded summary. This will provide advocacy partners (such as NAMI Iowa) with clear, block-level context on where operational gears are jammed heading into the next legislative session.
