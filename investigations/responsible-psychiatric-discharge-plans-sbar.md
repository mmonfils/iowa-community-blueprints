# RESPONSIBLE PSYCHIATRIC DISCHARGE PLANS (HF 385)

**Subject:** NAMI Iowa 2026 Legislative Priority: Requiring responsible discharge plans for patients who have had a psychiatric hospitalization

**Peer Solution Blueprint:** Washington State Behavioral Health Administrative Services Organization (BH-ASO) model

## I. SITUATION
Iowa House File 385 passed the House chamber with a unanimous 89-0 bipartisan vote. This total consensus establishes the bill as pre-vetted legislation capable of breaking through historical partisan friction. The current legislative interim provides the optimal strategic window to resolve the funding stalemate holding the bill in the Senate Health and Human Services Committee before the January 2027 session begins.

## II. BACKGROUND
HF 385 mandates that prior to discharging an involuntarily committed psychiatric patient, facilities must coordinate a warm handoff with a regional Administrative Services Organization (ASO) and provide a mandatory 15-day supply of prescribed medications. 

The operational roadblock is entirely financial. When immediate Medicaid or commercial insurance is unavailable at discharge, facilities face immediate cash-flow liabilities. Legacy hospital networks and state budget directors remain locked over who absorbs the overhead of running these temporary, uncompensated medication pipelines.

## III. ASSESSMENT
The State of Washington structurally separates hospital discharge liabilities from regional administrative overhead through two explicit policy mechanics:
* **Centralized Non-Medicaid Flexible Risk Pools:** Washington allocates state-funded, non-Medicaid risk pools to act as regional financial shock absorbers. The temporary 15-day transitional medication bridge is billed directly to a state-reimbursed regional crisis stabilization fund explicitly earmarked for safety-net prescription coverage, bypassing localized hospital cash-flow disputes.
* **Pre-Discharge Pharmacy Networks & Direct Voucher Integration:** Care managers route transitional prescriptions directly to a pre-contracted regional network of pharmacies. These pharmacies operate under a standardized, state-approved first-fill voucher protocol. This treats the immediate 15-day supply as a contiguous component of the active crisis intervention window, bypassing institutional billing delays entirely.

*The Comparative Fiscal Reality:* Behavioral health data demonstrates a stark fiscal asymmetry. The immediate front-end cost of providing a short-term, 15-day transitional prescription buffer represents a minor, predictable allocation of regional safety-net funds. Conversely, an immediate clinical relapse driven by an interrupted medication pipeline directly manifests as high-intensity, back-end municipal liabilities: emergency department boarding, acute crisis responses, and immediate county jail intake costs. These outcomes extract significantly higher daily operational tolls from local and county budgets.

## IV. RECOMMENDATION
1. **Amend HF 385 Fiscal Infrastructure:** Introduce a legislative amendment establishing an Iowa Regional Crisis Stabilization Risk Pool within the existing ASO framework, modeled after the Washington BH-ASO mechanism, to absorb uncompensated 15-day post-discharge medication costs.
2. **Launch First-Fill Voucher Pilot:** Partner with regional pharmacy networks to deploy a standardized voucher protocol, routing transition bills directly away from hospital cash-flow lines.
3. **Deploy Asymmetric Cost Briefings:** Deliver targeted, block-level fiscal impact data to key Senate committee leadership, demonstrating how front-end safety-net investments directly mitigate back-end county jail and emergency room operational deficits.
