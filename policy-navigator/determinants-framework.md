# Neighborhood Impact Analysis: The Three Scales

Use this guide to break down complicated public rules, economic shifts, or outside corporate actions. This framework maps how massive global changes trickles down through official structures to impact the everyday reality of our local streets.

***

## 1. The Core Architecture

We look at any local neighborhood challenge by mapping it across five distinct pillars and tracking it through three physical scales.



### The Five Local Pillars
* **Economic Security:** Cost of living, local jobs, household poverty, and local food availability.
* **School Access:** Local classroom funding, reading levels, vocational trades, and early childhood care.
* **Health Infrastructure:** Physical distance to care clinics, health coverage, and local health literacy.
* **The Built Environment:** Neighborhood housing quality, transit routes, clean air and water, and street safety.
* **Social Connections:** Active voter turnout, local discrimination, nearby support networks, and working conditions.

### The Three Scales of Impact
* **The Wide Screen (Global Trends):** Borderless currents such as changing weather patterns, international trade wars, nationwide inflation, and automation.
* **The Middle Gear (National and State Rules):** Intermediary legal networks such as federal legislation, state spending budgets, corporate supply chains, and banking rules.
* **The Ground Level (Local Neighborhood Realities):** The exact spots where rules meet real people, including city zoning codes, local neighborhood maps, and municipal water quality.

***

## 2. Step-by-Step Implementation Guide

### Step 1: Isolate Your Main Pillars
Identify which of the five pillars are actively causing friction in your local community. Focus your energy on one to three pillars that have the highest immediate impact on your problem rather than tracking all five areas at once.

### Step 2: Choose Your Starting Point
You can look at a local problem by starting from the top down, or by working from the ground up.

#### Option A: The Top-Down Track
Use this track when analyzing a massive national trend, a newly passed federal law, or a major technological shift.
1. **Name the Trend:** State the big outside development (e.g., "Nationwide, we are seeing massive inflation...").
2. **Track the Middle Gear:** Identify how state offices or large corporations react (e.g., "...which forces state budget cuts to local services...").
3. **Map the Local Impact:** Connect that reaction directly to your neighborhood corridor (e.g., "...which eliminates our neighborhood bus routes").

#### Option B: The Ground-Up Track
Use this track when analyzing an immediate neighborhood emergency, a local block complaint, or a troubling street metric.
1. **Name the Local Friction:** State the exact ground-level problem (e.g., "Neighbors on this block are seeing an spike in utility shut-offs...").
2. **Track the Middle Gear Barrier:** Look upward to find the official rules or utilities governing this issue (e.g., "...which is driven by state-level deregulation policies...").
3. **Map the Core Driver:** Connect those institutions to the wider economic forces (e.g., "...which is ultimately fueled by global energy supply shocks").

### Step 3: Locate Your Leverage Point
Trace the connections to find where a simple community action can break the chain. Determine if the ground-level problem requires an official rule change at the middle tier, or if building a localized neighborhood defense pod can shield residents from the outside trend entirely.

***

## 3. Communication Strategy: The Clear Narrative

When sharing your findings with neighbors, local steering committees, or outside supporters, keep your message organized, grounded, and practical.

* **Lead with Common Terms:** Organize your findings under the five standard pillars so everyone instantly understands the baseline categories.
* **Tell a Straightforward Story:** Present your final notes in a clean, sequential flow moving step-by-step from the wide screen down to the ground level.
* **Focus on the Solution:** Wrap up by pointing out your exact community leverage point to show that your neighborhood group is fixing structural causes rather than just chasing symptoms.
